John Deere watch app

A troubleshooting app on Apple Watch for JohnDeere customers who are baby boomers.

My Role

Responsible for user interviews, product strategies and all the design deliverables.

Tools & Methods

  • Sketch, Invision
  • Contextual Inquiries, Competitive Analysis, Personas, Mockups, Prototyping, Usability testing

    • Goal

    Identify a pain point and design an Apple Watch app to solve it for John Deere baby boomer users.

    Our Process

    Milestone 1: Define use cases

    What we did

  • Analyzed a few categories that John Deere has products in, and went with the Turfs category since we're located in Bay Area and don't have as many farms.
  • Interviewed with John Deere customers and dealers and learned about what pain points most customers experience and how they solve them.
  • Crafted 3 personas based on our data, and chose business owners as our target users because they're more likely to use an Apple Watch.
  • Based on the data collected from interviews and our persona, we came up with 3 use cases for the app - Overview, Troubleshooting and Performance.

    • What we learned

    It was extremely challenging when we first started, because baby boommers are usually not avid users of Apple Watch, and John Deere has a smaller customer base in Bay Area, which makes it difficult to find users to talk to in person.

  • We used both online and offline methods during our research. We interviewed customers and dealers as much as we could in person, and then we also did research online to understand customers' behaviors.
  • We learned to narrow down to only one product category after learning more about the company and considering our geographics constraint.
  • We didn't base our persona on our actual interviewees because average baby boomers might not need or be able to afford an apple watch. Therefore, we created a new persona based on collected data and our constraints.

    • Milestone 2: First design iteration & Design Review in class

    What we did

  • Developed the first version of the design for the watch app and presented in class.
  • The mockups below demonstrated the overview of machines, performance and troubleshooting use cases.
  • Created 3 versions of notifications for this app and we chose the transparent one.

    • What we learned

  • There were too many words (the Problem and Tips section) in the app, which doesn't align with the purpose of an apple watch.
  • Too many different colors and the patterns were not consistent, which could be confusing to users.
  • It felt like the app was missing a landing page that points out the most important information in the app or helps users navigate.

    • Milestone 3: Second design iteration & Usability testing

    What we did

  • Created a menu page as the landing page to help users better understand how to navigate in the app.
  • Replaced one green color with white for the content of the app.
  • Reduced the amount of words in the Troubleshooting use case using icons.
  • Created a new manual section for users to look up whenever they need since we added more icons
  • Conducted usability testing with 7+ users, including both business and individual owners.

    • What we learned

  • The way (our target persona) business owners manage their machines (behaviors) was different than we expected. It's more common business owners hire a contractor to manage the project and the superintendents who use the machines. Therefore, most business owners don't personally manage the machines at all.
  • Sometimes individual owners prefer to try to fix the machines themselves first.
  • The manual section did not align with the purpose of Apple Watch, because most users don't use it to acquire a large amount of knowledge.
  • Users mentioned the color scheme was confusing and not clean during interviews.

    • Milestone 4: Last design iteration & Usability testing

    What we did

  • Went back to using individual owners as our target users after understanding business owners' behaviors during usability testing.
  • Decided to focus on the Troubleshooting use case (both preventive and reactive) and notifications after modifying our persona and given the time constraint.
  • Created 3 rapid prototypes and ran testing with 5+ users.
  • Used the color orange yellow for the Schedule Now button to encourage users to schedule appointments with Dealers
  • We provided different caller options for different scenarios - Schedule a maintenance appointment with the dealer vs. Contact the technician to fix a part.
  • We provided both Tutorial and Contact Technician options for the troublshooting option based on the feedback from usability testing.

    • What we learned

  • The most important thing we learned in this iteration is how to use Rapid Prototyping properly and avoid the Sunk Cost fallacy. We created more than 3 prototypes over the course of 2 days, and each of them was significantly different than the previous one because we iterated based on the feedback from our usability testing each time.
  • It might be good to demonstrate the hands-off approach with the iPhone for the self troubleshooting use case.
  • The current landing page (the menu) can be improved in order to align with the Apple Watch design.

    • Takeaways

    Key Learnings

  • It's critical to validate our hypothesis on our target users early on in the process before we even started with the design process.
  • It's important to be familiar with the device (Apple Watch) during the process because that helped us understand the information architecture better and how to present information more effectively.
  • Given the geographics challenge, we learned how to conduct usability testing with different groups that might not have all the characterestics of the target users, because it was still helpful for us to identify fundamental UX/UI issues.
  • I've learned how to let go of sunk cost and not to be afraid of spending more time on creating new prototypes based on the feedback collected from usability testing.
  • The biggest takeway for me is how to design a product that solves an actual problem for users and aligns with the purpose of the device with a clear Information Architecture.

    • Next Steps

    Personally I plan to spend more time and work on this app as a side project after the class. Apple Watch is mainly for notifications, and it feels like our app is still trying to do too much for target users, which defeats the purpose of the device. Therefore, I plan to use more apple watch apps to be further familiar with the the expereince of the device and modify the design of our troubleshooting app to create a better user experience.

    Game of Votes

    A board game that depicts the 2016 US presidential election.

    My Role

    Created a non-digital game and iterated based on feedback from playtesting.

    Tools & Methods

  • Sketch
  • Level design, Mockups, Playtesting

    • Goal

    Apply the game design concepts I learn during the semester and create a non-digital game.

    Our Process

    First Iteration

    What I did

  • Determined the theme (Election) of the game and identified the elements needed for the theme, i.e. Money (Fundraising), Votes (Campaigning), etc.
  • Created a set of attack cards, a set of policy cards, a set of chance cards and downloaded a bunch of images to represent players.
  • For this iteration, I also borrowed coins from the class inventory and used them as money.

    • How to play

  • In each turn, each player has 2 phases, and they must be in orderPhase I then Phase II
  • In Phase I, each player can choose to Buy an attack card OR Campaign- spend money to draw a policy card to accumulate votes OR Fundraise- draw a card to get more money.
  • In Phase II, Use an attack card OR Use a take (chance) card to take a card from someone else OR Draw a chance card (If a player used a Cancel card on the player that just played, he or she cannot draw a chance card in this round).
  • Whoever has the highest number of votes at the end of the game wins.

    • What I learned

  • Some players mentioned that they felt like they learned a lot more about how the election works in the US after the game
  • It's necessary to add a board to the game since players fundraised and campaigned in different states, so it's helpful to visualize the location.
  • The game is still not in-depth enough in terms of opposition and decisions. More element could be added in order to allow players to make more strategic decisions.
  • The chance cards seemed too random and didn't align with the game.
  • The style of the cartoon images (for players) doesn't align from the visual design perspective.

    • Second Iteration

    What I did

  • Added a map and divided it into 6 regions.
  • Assigned 2 candidates' to each region and gave them more power (more votes/money) in their favorable(assigned) regions.
  • Created a score card that allows players to keep track of the votes they got in each region.
  • Removed the chance cards because they were too random based on the feedback
  • Assigned more strategies to the Die - if it's 1-3, players might get the same amount of damages from the attack card; if it's 4-6, players might get less damages depending on if they are in their own region.

    • What I learned

  • Players suggested they be given a chance to use a die to increase the number of votes or money they receive from campaigning or fundraising in favorable regions,
  • Some of the policy cards were too strong and the number of votes on each card needed to be adjusted.
  • Players reflected that they didnt have the incentive to move to another region or fundraise because they wanted to always stay in their favorable regions.
  • The score card was difficult to use because it's unclear what the letter stands for.
  • Players felt they should be allowed to take more actions in each turn if they have enough money.
  • There are too many words on a card and it's important to highlight the element that players care about, i.e. votes or money.

    • Third Iteration

    What I did

  • Renamed the 2 phases to Clean Phase (Phase I) and Dirty Phase (Phase II) to make it more theatrical.
  • Created smaller maps for each player as a score card to keep track of the votes.
  • Added more power to the die and applied the same strategy for both money and votes.
  • Re-designed the attack and policy cards - if players are interested in learning the story, they can read it at the bottom of the card during downtime.
  • Used new and consistent styles to represent players in the game.
  • Used fake $1M dollar bill to get players more excited and immerse into the game better.

    • How to play (New rules)

  • Move the buy an attack card action to the dirty phase so that players can always attack in the same turnthis not only allows players to have more actions in one turn and it actually resonates with the actual election.
  • When players get attacked, their money/votes will be given to the attacker (instead of the bank) directly because it helped to move the game forward faster.
  • Each player has his or her own deck of policy cards because they are all based on true stories occurring in the current election.
  • Check for victory - each player has to get the highest number of votes within the party, and then the winners from their own party will compare and see who has the highest number of votes and will win the election.

    • What I learned

  • Come up with a more efficient way to balance the map, so its more fair to players when they move around the regions.
  • Figure out a way to make the attacks more relevant to the candidates.
  • The game can be easily outdated because things move fast during an election and it wont matter as much after it ends.

    • Takeaways

    Making a game is more challenging than I thought. When I started this class, I thought developing a game is similar to building a product, and thats why I wanted to take the class in the first place. Although they do share certain principles, little do I know how difficult it can be to make a game. If we just look at the mechanism of a game, its extremely critical for game designers to balance elements like obstacles and opposition/interactions in a game to at least make the game playable. Different than a product, a game is usually based on a theme or story, and game designers need to have the relevant components and the immersive design to make players feel the game is connected to the story, which is the most important thing I have learned by doing this project. People look for different kinds of fun when they play the game. If game designers want to attract as many players as possible to their game, they need to make it as engaging as possible for different types of players, which is even more challenging.

    BridgeTech

    A product that helps startups to manage contractors more effectively by tackling issues identified from recruiting to the end of the contract.

    My Role

    Product Lead, responsible for all aspects of the project and leading the team during the process.

    Tools & Methods

  • Storyboardthat, Sketch
  • User Research, Fishbone analysis, Competition Matrix, Personas, Storyboarding, URD, Mockups

    • Goal

    Find a solution to solve a problem in HR/Recruiting and validate it with users.

    Our Process

    Milestone 1: Discovery Interviews

    What we did

    For this iteration, we were initially given the Job Sharing topic to focus on for this project.

  • Interviewed with 15 users ranging from CEOs, recruiters, HR managers, employees of both large corporations and startups to students and contractors.
  • Identified 7 different ideas and chose one problem statement that has more growth potential.

    • What we learned

  • After the discovery interviews, users mentioned different pain points in recruiting/HR ranging from difficulties in hiring internationals, challenges when managing contractors, getting back into the job market after a break, etc.
  • We realized most interviewees/companies regardless of the company size are opposed to the job sharing method.
  • Then we decided to pivot and select the contractor management problem to focus on because about 50% of the interviewees (small and medium size of companies) reflect the same problem during our discovery interviews , and we have more access to target users in the Bay Area, which would be helpful to us for the validation process later.

    • Milestone 2: Solution & Personas

    What we did

  • Used the Fishbone diagram to conduct the Root Cause analysis on the selected idea.
  • Came up with a potential solution BridgeTech to solve the selected problem - help startups hire and manage contractors more effectively.
  • Crafted two personas that fit as the target users of the potential solution, and one represents Contractors and the other represents Startups.

    • What we learned

  • From the company's standpoint, it seemed that sometimes contractors' deliverables were different or lower than the expectation. Therefore, companies wasted the resources on recruiting and paying them, but failed to get what they needed from the contractors.
  • One of the problems with using contractors is that some companies have a much simpler process for hiring contractors, which led to the low quality of deliverables in the later stage.
  • Another pain point is that companies don't have a comprehensive project management process in place to work with contractors efficiently. Sometimes it's unclear to the contractors what the company is looking for in a deliverable, and then with more inefficient communication, the deliverable turns out to be something different than what the company has in mind.
  • After further analysis, we decided to focus on contractor management for only engineering and design roles at tech companies for this project instead, because most smaller companies only hire contractors for additional help in these fields, and the solution will be more valuable to them when hiring this type of contractors.

    • Milestone 3: Use cases

    What we did

  • Identified 5 use cases for the contractor management solution - Interview Design, Interview Scheduling, Project Design, Communication System and Analytics Reports.
  • Created Storyboards to describe the user journey.
  • Created Product Requirements Documents for the purpose of development.

    • What we learned

  • Based on the feedback, it's always good to include at least one alternative flow for the user journey for the same use case to fully consider all possible scenarios.
  • Personally, I found it extremely critical to identify the action points in each flow when using storyboarding to describe the user journey.

    • Milestone 4: Competitive Analysis

    What we did

  • Created a Petal Diagram to analyze our main competitors.
  • Created a Competition Matrix (a modified version based on our project's needs) to identify what our main competitors are.

    • What we learned

  • We spent a lot of time on researching on all the companies that might share similar features with our solution and created 4 different versions (only 2 versions were presented above).
  • As we learned more and more about our competitors in different sectors, we narrowed down to Lystable and WorkMarket as our key competitors.
  • While there might be still room for BridgeTech to grow, we realized the competition is very fierce in this domain.

    • Milestone 5: Validations & Our Decision

    What we did

  • Interviewed with 2 CEOs of startups and 3 contractors.
  • Made a decision on conducting more research on this idea (the decision was required at the end of the class and it could be Yes, No or More Research).

    • What we learned

  • According to one of the CEOs we interviewed with, startups are usually more scrappy and opposed to implementing too much process at the workplace, so it could be challenging to convince startups to get on board and implement BridgeTech.
  • Contractors mention sometimes they have bigger problems to worry about such as if they work for the right client (when they can choose) or they can receive payment on time, so BridgeTech doesn't solve their biggest pain point.
  • Therefore, even though it seems that there's a problem with the way startups manage contractors, we felt like we needed to spend more time on researching and further validating our solution with target users.

    • Takeaways

    Key Learnings

  • During the project, we conducted a few rounds of user interviews with target users and pivoted a few times based on the feedback. I certainly have come to understand the importance of validating the idea in the early stage of the product development process.
  • I had the opportunity to use different tools and methods during this process such as storyboards, PRD, Fishbone diagram and the Petal diagram. It helped me to understand what tools/methods I should use and how to modify them based on our needs.
  • It was also a great opportunity for me to be the Product Lead on this project, because it helped me to significantly improve my leadership skills on how to manage team members' differences, prioritize our tasks for the team during each iteration, adjust to changes during the process, and guidethe team towards our goal.

    • Next Steps

    Personally I plan to select a few use cases and create mockups of the features as my personal design project, so stay tuned!
